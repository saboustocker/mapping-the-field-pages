A.1.1 Inclusion Pipeline

All \(n = 10{,}196\) papers retained after deduplication were screened for whether they discussed a technology, using gpt-4.1-mini (OpenAI API, temperature \(= 0\), JSON-schema-constrained structured output). The classifier received each paper’s title and abstract and returned three fields: a boolean indicating whether a technology was discussed, the name of the technology using the authors’ own terminology (verbatim from the title or abstract, rather than a paraphrase), and a one-sentence justification. Papers naming multiple technologies had all of them listed, separated by semicolons; the prompt instructed the classifier to default to non-inclusion under uncertainty and to require an explicit, specific technology rather than a generic term such as “innovation” or “platform.” The classifier was piloted on a random subset of 500 papers before being applied to the remaining corpus. Papers with multiple named technologies were subsequently split into one row per candidate technology mention for downstream processing. The prompt used for the first screening is provided below.

NotePrompt: Technology screening
# Prompt
PROMPT_TECHNOLOGY = ““”

You must classify papers based on their title and abstract into whether they mention a **technology** or not. The mention of the technology can be both as the focal object of the research, or as an instrumental tool used in this research.

Technologies are scientific knowledge and/or its application for firms and/or consumers with the potential to influence the activity, institutions, and processes for creating, communicating, delivering, and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners, and society at large.

Rules:
- Use only the title and abstract.
- Do not infer technologies that are not explicitly mentioned.
- Use the same words as the authors in your response, do not rephrase their description
- A technology must be named in the title or abstract, either using the word “technology” to describe it or describing a specific technology directly. Generic words such as “innovation” or “platform” alone are not sufficient
- If multiple technologies are mentioned, list all of them and separate with a “;”
- If discusses_technology is FALSE, set name_technology to an empty string ““.
- If uncertain, choose false.

Output fields:
- discusses_technology: TRUE if a technology is explicitly mentioned, otherwise FALSE.
- name_technology: the technology name using the exact terminology from the title/abstract; empty string if FALSE.
- justification_technology: one brief sentence explaining the classification.

Return JSON only.”“”

A.1.2 Exclusion Pipeline

Independently of the first pass through an inclusion pipeline, all papers where passed through a second, independently prompted gpt-4.1-mini classifier (temperature \(= 0\), structured JSON output) instructed to critically evaluate whether each candidate term was genuinely a technology, rather than a marketing practice, communication format, consumer behavior, or generic/vague term (e.g., “platform,” “digital,” “innovation” without a named referent). The prompt supplied explicit examples of terms to reject (e.g., influencer marketing, product placement, branding) and to accept (e.g., mobile payment apps, recommendation algorithms, blockchain), and required the model to return a justification, a confidence rating, and, for rejected candidates, a rejection category (marketing practice, consumer behavior, business concept, generic term, not explicit, too ambiguous, or other). Papers the screening stage had classified as not discussing any technology were carried through this stage as well, so that the two stages’ classifications could be directly compared for agreement. All papers where the two pipelines disagreed were evaluated by a human rater. The prompt used for the exclusionary pass is provided below.

NotePrompt: Technology validation
PROMPT_VALIDATE_TECHNOLOGY = ““”

You are a skeptical validator.

Your task is NOT to be helpful, generous, or inclusive. Your task is to critically check whether this paper is truly a technology according to the definition.

Definition:
A technology is the application of conceptual knowledge to achieve practical goals in a reproducible way. Technologies include concrete tools, devices, machines, technical systems, software, algorithms, digital infrastructures, platforms, or engineered methods that function as tools or products.

Reject the candidate if it is mainly:
- a marketing strategy, tactic, or practice rather than a tool or system
- a communication format, media content, campaign type, or promotional technique
- a consumer behavior, social phenomenon, or managerial activity
- a broad business concept such as innovation, product placement, influencer marketing, branding, advertising, word of mouth, loyalty programs, customer engagement, personalization, or social interaction, unless the term explicitly names a concrete technological system, software, digital platform, algorithm, device, or technical infrastructure
- a vague word such as “platform”, “digital”, “online”, “mobile”, “virtual”, “innovation”, or “technology” without a specific named technology
- merely the context in which a study occurs, rather than the tool/product/system being studied or used. If the technology is explicitly used as a method, do not reject.

Examples that should usually be rejected:
- influencer marketing
- product placement
- advertising
- online advertising
- social media marketing
- electronic word of mouth
- branding
- personalization
- mobile shopping

Examples that may be accepted:
- mobile payment apps
- recommendation algorithms
- artificial intelligence
- augmented reality
- virtual reality
- blockchain
- chatbots
- social media platforms, if the platform/system itself is explicitly studied as a technology
- mobile apps
- self-service technologies
- facial recognition technology
- wearable devices
- online review platforms, if the platform is explicitly the technical system under study

Rules:
- Use only the title and abstract.
- Do not infer technologies that are not explicitly mentioned.
- Be stricter than the first classifier.
- If the candidate is ambiguous, choose false.
- If the candidate is a practice enabled by technology, but not itself a technology, choose false.
- If the candidate is too broad or generic, choose false.
- If false, explain briefly why it is not a technology.
- If true, explain briefly why it qualifies as a technology.
- Do not replace the candidate with a better term unless the better term appears explicitly in the title or abstract.
- If there is a more precise technology term explicitly present, provide it in revised_technology_name.
- Otherwise, revised_technology_name should equal the original candidate term if true, or an empty string if false.

Output fields:
- validated_technology: TRUE if the candidate itself is truly a technology, otherwise FALSE.
- revised_technology_name: the accepted technology name using exact wording from the title/abstract; empty string if FALSE.
- validation_reason: one brief sentence explaining the decision.
- rejection_category: if FALSE, choose one of: “marketing_practice”, “consumer_behavior”, “business_concept”, “generic_term”, “not_explicit”, “too_ambiguous”, “other”; if TRUE, use “accepted”.
- confidence: “high”, “medium”, or “low”.

Return JSON only.

““”

A.1.3 Identifying Consumer-facing and Empirical Papers

Papers retained after human-reconciled review of the screening and validation stages were evaluated on three further dimensions using a third gpt-4.1-mini classifier (temperature \(= 0\), structured JSON output): whether the candidate technology was consumer-facing, defined as directly used by, shown to, or experienced by consumers or end users; whether the paper was empirical, i.e., relied on data of any kind (experimental, secondary, meta-analytic, or big data) rather than being purely conceptual or theoretical. The classifier received each paper’s title, abstract, the candidate technology label, and the publication year, and returned a boolean judgment, a brief justification, and a confidence rating for each of the dimensions. 20% of the results from this iteration were independently validated by a human rater, classifying the abstract on the same dimensions and according to the same criteria, resulting in an overlap between the human and LLM rater of 91%. The prompt used to identify consumer-facing technologies and empirical papers is provided below.

Note that the prompt also classifies the subjective novelty of each consumer-facing technology. We decided against including this exploratory measure in our analysis, but retain it in the prompt to report our process transparently.

NotePrompt: Consumer-facing and empirical classification
PROMPT_CONSUMER_TECH_EMPIRICAL_NOVELTY = ““”
You are evaluating technology mentions in academic paper titles and abstracts.

Evaluate the candidate technology individually on the following dimensions:

Dimension 1: consumer_tech

Question:
Is the candidate technology consumer-facing?

Definition:
A consumer-facing technology is a technology that consumers / end users directly interact with or directly experience.

Mark consumer_tech as true if the technology is used by, shown to, interacted with, or directly experienced by consumers or end users.

Mark consumer_tech as false otherwise.

Dimension 2: empirical

Question:
Does the paper rely on data in any form, or is it purely conceptual?

Mark empirical as true if the abstract indicates that the paper relies on data in any form, including experimental, secondary analysis, meta-analysis, or big data.

Mark empirical as false if the paper is purely conceptual, theoretical,
argumentative, editorial, or a literature/framework paper without data.

Dimension 3: novel_at_publication

Question:
Was the candidate technology new at the time of publication?

Definition:
“New” refers to recent applications of scientific knowledge that have not been
replaced by others. In other words, technology is “new” when it is early in the
adoption cycle for firms and/or consumers, meaning in the innovator or early
adopter phase.

Mark novel_at_publication as true if, based on the title, abstract, candidate
technology, and publication year, the technology appears to be “new” as defined above at the time of publication.

Mark novel_at_publication as false if the technology is not “new” according to the definition above.

Important rules:
- Use only the title, abstract, candidate technology, publication year, and first-round justification if provided.
- Do not infer information that is not supported by the title or abstract.
- Return JSON only.
““”

A.2 Technology Deduplication and Clustering

Technology labels retained after all inclusion criteria were first lexically normalized: lowercased, stripped of leading articles and parenthetical abbreviations, and cleaned of punctuation and repeated whitespace; simple plural forms were collapsed onto their singular equivalent where both forms occurred in the label set. Semantically near-duplicate labels were then merged using all-MiniLM-L6-v2 sentence embeddings: each unique normalized label was embedded, and pairs with cosine similarity \(\geq 0.90\) were merged via a union-find procedure into equivalence classes, with the member closest on average to the rest of its class retained as the canonical label. The rationale for this embedding model, similarity threshold, and the subsequent choice of \(k\) is reported in chapter sec-technology-clustering of the supplementary materials. Canonical technology labels were aggregated to one row per unique technology, re-embedded, and reduced from 384 to 10 dimensions via UMAP (cosine metric, minimum distance \(= 0\)) for clustering, and separately to 2 dimensions (minimum distance \(= 0.05\)) for visualization. \(k\)-means clustering (\(k = 16\), 50 restarts) was applied to the 10-dimensional embeddings to assign each unique technology to one of 16 clusters.
