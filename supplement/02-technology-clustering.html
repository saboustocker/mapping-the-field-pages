Appendix B — Technology Clustering: Model Selection and Fit

To fit the technology-clustering model reported in the main text, we swept the embedding model, the deduplication threshold, the number of clusters (\(k\)), and the number of UMAP dimensions used for clustering, evaluating each combination on silhouette, Davies-Bouldin, and Calinski-Harabasz scores, and, for the dimensionality sweep, trustworthiness against the original embedding space.

B.1 Embedding model

We compared three sentence-embedding models — sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2, BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5, and nomic-ai/nomic-embed-text-v1.5 — across every tested combination of \(k\) and deduplication threshold (Table tbl-model-comparison). all-MiniLM-L6-v2 achieved the highest mean silhouette score overall (0.426), ahead of BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5 (0.41) and nomic-ai/nomic-embed-text-v1.5 (0.394), and was therefore selected as the embedding model for the final clustering solution.

B.2 Number of clusters (k) and deduplication threshold (d)

Holding the embedding model and deduplication threshold fixed, silhouette scores rose gradually across the swept range of \(k = 10\) to \(k = 20\), without a clear inflection point marking a single statistical optimum (Table tbl-k-comparison): \(k = 16\) reached a silhouette of 0.431, the next value tested (\(k = 17\)) offered only a marginal improvement (0.436), and the score continued to climb gradually through the top of the swept range (\(k = 20\): 0.473). Because the statistical criteria did not identify a single unambiguous optimum, we prioritized interpretability: \(k = 16\) produced clusters that mapped onto substantively distinct, non-overlapping technology domains upon manual inspection, and we report it as the final solution while acknowledging that \(k = 17\) was marginally better by silhouette alone.

We also swept the deduplication threshold (\(d\)) used to collapse near-duplicate technology labels prior to clustering. Cluster fit statistics did not favor a stricter threshold: at the chosen model and \(k = 16\) (Table tbl-threshold-comparison), \(d = 0.92\) reached the highest silhouette (0.445), \(d = 0.90\) reached 0.431, and \(d = 0.95\) performed worst of the three (0.424). More importantly, raising the threshold did not translate into more meaningful deduplication in practice: even at \(d = 0.95\), semantically identical near-duplicate labels such as “smartphone” and “smart phone” remained separate, unmerged technologies. Because a stricter threshold neither reliably improved cluster fit nor produced more meaningful merges, we retained \(d = 0.90\) as the deduplication threshold for the final clustering solution.

B.3 Dimensionality (UMAP components)

For the number of UMAP dimensions used in clustering, we evaluated trustworthiness — how well the reduced space preserves the original embedding space’s local neighborhood structure — alongside the cluster fit statistics, at the chosen model, threshold, and \(k = 16\) (Table tbl-dim-comparison). Trustworthiness rose sharply from 2 to 5 dimensions and then plateaued through 50 dimensions (0.923 at 5 dimensions, 0.924 at 10, 0.92 at 50), while silhouette and Davies-Bouldin scores at the lowest dimensionality tested (2 components) were inflated relative to this plateau — a well-documented tendency of clustering metrics to overstate separation in very low-dimensional projections [1–3]. We selected 10 dimensions as the point at which the reduced space had already captured the local structure of the original embeddings, without the artificial separation inflating fit statistics at the lowest dimensionalities tested.

B.4 Cluster Visualization and Temporal Analysis

Cluster assignments were merged back onto the paper-level dataset via the canonical technology label, restricted to consumer-facing, empirical mentions, and clusters were manually labelled based on their constituent technologies. Publication years were grouped into two-year intervals spanning 2000–2026, anchored to the most recent year with any remaining span absorbed into the earliest interval, and article counts per cluster were tabulated across intervals. Three temporal visualizations were produced from these tabulations: a line chart of raw article counts per cluster over time, a 100%-stacked-area chart of each cluster’s share of articles per interval (overlaid with the total article count per interval), and an absolute stacked-area chart of article counts per interval. A semantic overlay map, analogous to a VOSviewer network map, was also produced: each technology was plotted at its two-dimensional UMAP coordinates, sized by mention frequency and colored by cluster, alongside cluster centroids positioned at the frequency-weighted mean coordinates of their constituent technologies and sized by total cluster frequency.
