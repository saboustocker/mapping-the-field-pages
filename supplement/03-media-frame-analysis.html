Appendix D — Media Frame Analysis: Analysis Pipeline

We analyzed newspaper coverage of the 901 canonical technologies identified in the academic corpus. For this analysis, we used Media Cloud [1], an open web archive of news content.

D.1 Media Cloud Search Construction

For each canonical technology, we generated a title-restricted Media Cloud query and a corresponding Python regular expression using gpt-4.1-mini through the OpenAI API (\(T = 0\); JSON-schema-constrained structured output). We constructed the Media Cloud queries from wildcard-prefixed terms joined with AND and OR operators and restricted the search to article titles using the article_title:(...) syntax.

Because Media Cloud’s title-search operator does not enforce word adjacency or word order, we used the corresponding regular expression to filter the retrieved titles locally. The regular expression required the constituent words of each technology term to occur directly next to one another and in the intended order. This procedure ensured that the retained titles reflected the technology terms used in the academic corpus. The resulting search strings and regular expressions are reported in Table D.1.

We executed each query against Media Cloud’s United States – National collection (collection ID 34412234), which contained 247 national U.S. news sources. The included sources are listed in Table D.2. The searches covered the period from January 1, 2000, through June 30, 2026, and retrieved all candidate stories whose titles matched the corresponding Media Cloud query.

We retained only stories whose titles also matched the locally applied regular expression and discarded the remaining broad-query matches as false positives. We then aggregated the retained stories into annual mention counts. We stored the matched records separately for each technology term in individual .csv files and also combined them into an aggregated dataset for analyses across technologies and years.

D.2 Cluster-Level Aggregation and Visualization

We summed the annual mention counts for the individual technologies within their assigned clusters, producing annual newspaper-mention counts for each of the 16 technology clusters used in the academic analysis.

Table D.3 summarizes the number of mentions aggregated across technology clusters. Terms from the Social Media cluster appeared most frequently (\(n = 1,022,372\)), followed by Internet & Broadcasting (\(n = 801,145\)) and Other (\(n = 517,395\)). The Other cluster increased sharply in 2021–2022, coinciding with increased coverage of healthcare technologies and vaccines during the COVID-19 pandemic. The least frequently covered clusters were Auctions & Pricing (\(n = 1,076\)), Tracking & Wearables (\(n = 25,527\)), and Mobility & Transportation (\(n = 34,812\)).

The searches covered the full period from 2000 through 2026 to retain all historically indexed records. However, Media Cloud reports that systematic data collection began in 2008. Coverage of individual news sources generally begins when Media Cloud started ingesting their syndicated feeds, supplemented by sitemap data and subsequently added historical records [2]. Because coverage before 2008 is sparse and less reliable [1], we restricted all descriptive and statistical analyses to records published from 2008 onward.

Figure D.1, Figure D.2, and Figure D.3 visualize this cluster-level aggregation over the 2008–2026 period. The interactive HTML versions let readers inspect individual clusters and time periods in more detail than the static summary in Table D.3 allows.

Together, Figure D.1, Figure D.2, and Figure D.3 show how newspaper attention to the technology clusters compares in spatial composition, scale, and relative emphasis with the academic corpus described in the main manuscript and in Appendix B.

D.3 Mention-Level Overlap Across Technology Terms

Distinct technology terms could retrieve the same newspaper story through separate, partially overlapping Media Cloud queries. We therefore quantified overlap directly from the retained story records. For each technology term, we counted both the number of retained term–story matches and the number of unique articles identified by their Media Cloud story identifier.

When estimating the total number of articles in the media corpus, we counted each unique story only once, even when it matched multiple technology terms. For mention-level and cluster-level analyses, we retained each valid term–story match because a single article could substantively discuss more than one technology. We assessed the extent of overlap by comparing the sum of retained term-level matches with the number of distinct stories matched by at least one technology term.
